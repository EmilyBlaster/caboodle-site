chapter i · approach

the whole caboodle.

Where strategy, media, and behavioral science converge. Three methods, applied in order, borrowed from anthropology, behavioral science, and editorial media. Not from the LMS vendor catalog.

01
ethnographic research

I observe what people actually do before I design anything.

Before a single storyboard, I study what people actually do in their workplace context. This anthropological approach ensures training addresses real behavior, not assumptions. I watch. I listen. I ask the uncomfortable questions.

  • contextual inquiry · stakeholder interviews · artifact analysis
  • global & compliance: localization, wcag, regulatory standards
  • curriculum architecture: outcome-driven learning paths
02
behavioral strategy

I design for behavior change. Then I measure it.

Action Mapping for design. A layered Kirkpatrick-Phillips evaluation model for measurement, from learner reaction all the way to demonstrated ROI for the business. Nudges, motivation design, habit loops, AI integration. The thinking is the job.

  • action mapping · kirkpatrick-phillips · choice architecture
  • measurement instrumented before launch, not after
  • ai as a field tool, never as a substitution
03
creative media

Scalable learning systems, not one-off decks.

Interaction design, video, motion graphics, visual storytelling. Design systems and media libraries and interactive experiences. Every asset is built to evolve with your organization, so the training investment future-proofs itself.

  • remotion · three.js · storyline · rise · editorial web
  • single source of truth asset systems
  • accessibility and retention measured, not assumed

chapter iv · about emily

14+ years in learning design.
one stubborn conviction: training is the last resort, not the first.

I'm Emily Green. A certified Google UX professional and learning experience designer with over a decade of experience in graphic, instructional, and visual design leadership. I create visually engaging and interactive learning experiences for public and private organizations.

Before I design anything, I study how people actually behave at work. I watch. I listen. I ask the uncomfortable questions. Because the gap between what we trained them to do and what they actually do is where the real design challenge lives.

I started caboodle design because I was tired of seeing organizations pour resources into training that checked a box but never changed a behavior. My work sits at the intersection of behavioral science, media production, and instructional strategy. It's the whole caboodle.
